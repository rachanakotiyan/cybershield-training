Social Engineering & Offline Threats

Locked

Non-Technical Threats

Social Engineering is the art of manipulating employees so they give up access credentials or physical access keys. Attackers exploit helpfulness, fear, or simple curiosity:

Pretexting

The attacker invents a scenario (the pretext) and impersonates a trusted authority (e.g. corporate auditors, HVAC engineers, IT technicians) to request access codes.

Baiting

Attackers leave a physical device (e.g. malware-loaded USB flash drive) labeled with an intriguing title like "Bonus Spreadsheet" in public breakout rooms, hoping a curious employee plugs it in.

Tailgating

An unauthorized intruder walks closely behind an authorized employee entering a badge-secured facility, bypassing physical access barriers.

Defensive Behaviors

Since social engineering targets your habits and reactions, your daily actions are your best defense:

1. The "Verify First" Rule

Never hand over company files, access logs, or details about software to an unexpected caller. Ask for their name, hang up, and call them back using the official numbers listed in your company catalog.

2. Badge Discipline

Every single employee must swipe their badge to unlock security doors. If a visitor is carrying boxes or has their hands full, offer to hold their items while they swipe, or guide them to the reception desk.

3. Lock Your Screen

Always press Windows Key + L (or Ctrl + Cmd + Q on Mac) when leaving your workspace. An unlocked workstation is an open invitation for physical data theft.

Scenario Simulation Exercises

Select the safest response to these real-world security challenges:

SCENARIO 1: The Heavy Box Carrier PHYSICAL ENTRY

You are entering the office through a badge-secured security door. An unknown person carrying a heavy stack of cardboard boxes walks closely behind you and asks, "Could you hold the door for me?"

SCENARIO 2: The Urgent IT Call VISHING INTRUSION

A caller claiming to be "Mark from corporate network engineering" states that a data leak has been traced to your PC. He says he needs you to read back the 6-digit MFA confirmation code sent to your phone immediately to verify your identity.

SCENARIO 3: The Parking Lot Flash Drive BAITING ATTEMPT

In the corporate parking lot, you spot a USB flash drive sitting on the asphalt. The plastic shell has a handwritten label: "Staff Layoffs & Compensation Q4".
