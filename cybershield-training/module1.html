Understanding Phishing

Phishing is a method where cybercriminals impersonate reputable organizations or colleagues to trick employees into sharing sensitive data (like passwords or banking routing codes) or executing malicious files.

Why is it called Phishing?

Just like a fisherman drops a baited hook into the water hoping a fish will bite, attackers send millions of emails containing "hooks" hoping someone will click.

How Attacks Exploit Psychology

Attackers build trust or panic by using psychological triggers:

  • Urgency: "Verify your password in 2 hours or your account is suspended."
  • Authority: "This is the CEO, I need you to wire funds immediately."
  • Curiosity: "View your Q3 bonus proposal sheet here."

Forms of Phishing

Phishing is not limited to generic spam emails. Attackers tailor their methods based on their specific targets:

Spear Phishing

A highly targeted email attack aimed at a specific individual or department. Attackers gather personal details from social networks to make the scam highly convincing.

Whaling

Spear phishing directed specifically at high-level executives (CEO, CFO). The goals usually involve executive credentials or authorizing major bank transfers.

Smishing & Vishing

Phishing conducted via text messages (Smishing) or telephone calls (Vishing). Attackers might claim there's a fraud alert on your card or that IT needs an verification PIN.

Clone Phishing

An attacker copies a legitimate email that you previously received, replaces the link or attachment with a malicious one, and sends it from a look-alike address.
