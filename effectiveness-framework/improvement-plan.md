# Security Awareness Continuous Improvement Plan

A security program must adapt to changing employee performance data and a rapidly evolving cyber threat landscape. This document outlines how to analyze program results, iterate the training curriculum, and address persistent compliance issues.

---

## 1. The Feedback and Iteration Loop

The program is structured as a continuous loop. Data from simulations, assessments, and real incident reports should directly inform the next cycle of training.

```
[Analyze Program Metrics] ---> [Identify Risk Areas] ---> [Update Curriculum/Drills] ---> [Rollout Next Cycle]
```

### Review Cadence:
*   **Monthly Review:** IT/Security and HR review real phishing reports and helpdesk incidents to check for new attack vectors targeting the company.
*   **Quarterly Review:** Evaluate simulation click/reporting rates and desk audit results.
*   **Annual Review:** Analyze pre- vs. post-assessment data and overall program completion rates to present to executive leadership.

---

## 2. Managing "Repeat Clickers" (Persistent Failure Protocol)

In every organization, a small percentage of employees will fail phishing simulations multiple times. Rather than using punitive measures immediately, the compliance manager must use a progressive, educational remediation scale.

### The Progressive Remediation Framework:

```
1st Failure:   Friendly Landing Page (Quick Tips)
2nd Failure:   Automated 15-Minute Micro-Learning Module Assigned
3rd Failure:   One-on-One Session with Security Team & Supervisor Sync
4th Failure:   HR Review / Official Security Policy Notice
```

#### Step 1: First Simulation Failure
*   **Action:** Direct the employee to the standard simulation landing page containing quick tips on what red flags were missed.
*   **Tone:** Educational and positive.

#### Step 2: Second Simulation Failure (Within 12 Months)
*   **Action:** Assign a mandatory, short (10-15 min) video module focusing on email scrutiny.
*   **Timeline:** Employee must complete it within 5 working days.

#### Step 3: Third Simulation Failure (Within 12 Months)
*   **Action:** A brief (15-20 min) live one-on-one session with the security manager to review how the employee checks their email. The employee's immediate supervisor is notified.
*   **Goal:** Discover if there is a workflow issue (e.g., the employee is rushed, lacks tools, or has visual difficulty checking links).

#### Step 4: Fourth Simulation Failure (Within 12 Months)
*   **Action:** Referral to HR for policy non-compliance review.
*   **Consequence:** Formal performance note or temporary restriction of external email privileges until a comprehensive certification assessment is passed.

## 3. Adapting to Evolving Threat Landscapes

Cybercriminals change their tactics frequently. The training program should be updated annually to include modern attack methods.

### Current Threats to Add in Next Updates:

#### A. QR Code Phishing ("Quishing")
*   **The Attack:** Attackers place QR codes in emails (or physical stickers in offices) leading to malicious sites. Security email filters cannot easily scan QR codes, allowing them to bypass technical shields.
*   **Training Update:** Teach employees never to scan QR codes in emails, especially those asking for passwords or claiming to setup multi-factor authentication.

#### B. Artificial Intelligence & Deepfakes (Vishing/Video)
*   **The Attack:** Attackers use AI to clone the voice of the CEO or a key vendor, calling Finance to request urgent fund transfers.
*   **Training Update:** Establish strict "out-of-band verification" protocols. If a voice call from a manager requests an unusual financial transaction, the employee must contact that manager via a different known channel (e.g., Teams chat) to verify, regardless of how realistic the voice sounds.

#### C. Collaborative Tool Phishing (Teams / Slack)
*   **The Attack:** Compromised guest accounts or external contacts sending malicious links/files directly via Microsoft Teams or Slack.
*   **Training Update:** Remind employees that phishing is not limited to email. Treat external Slack/Teams links with the same caution as email links.
