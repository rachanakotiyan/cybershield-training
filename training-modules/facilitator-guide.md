# Facilitator Guide: Security Awareness Training

This guide is designed for HR, Compliance, or IT managers to conduct live, interactive security awareness training sessions. Use this document to prepare for and lead a 45-to-60 minute session that reinforces the core training modules.

---

## 1. Preparation Checklist
Before scheduling a session, ensure you have completed the following steps:
- [ ] **Book the Venue/Platform:** Secure a meeting room with a projector or set up a corporate video conferencing link (Teams, Zoom, etc.).
- [ ] **Review Materials:** Read through Modules 1, 2, and 3 to ensure you understand the concepts and answer keys.
- [ ] **Prepare Slides:** Draft a slide deck based on the outline in Section 3 of this guide.
- [ ] **Gather Handouts:** Print copies of the "Red Flags Checklist" (from Module 1) or share the PDF link via email ahead of the session.
- [ ] **Prepare Activity Props:** If doing the live physical activities, print out the sample phishing emails (found in the "Spot the Phish" activity) or bring a blank notepad.

## 3. Speaking Points & Slide Outline

### Slide 1: Title & Welcome
*   **Slide Content:** "Building a Human Firewall: Our Security Awareness Program." Facilitator name and contact details.
*   **Talking Points:** Welcome everyone. Cybersecurity isn't just an IT issue; it's a critical business risk that relies on all of us. Explain that 90%+ of corporate data breaches start with a simple human action (like clicking a link). We are here to empower you, not test you.

### Slide 2: Phishing Fundamentals
*   **Slide Content:** Definition of phishing, psychological triggers (Urgency, Authority, Fear).
*   **Talking Points:** Explain that phishing works by tricking our emotions. If an email makes you feel panicked, excited, or scared, that is your cue to pause. Attackers want you to react before you think.

### Slide 3: Spotting the Scam
*   **Slide Content:** Hovering over links, looking closely at sender email headers, generic greetings.
*   **Talking Points:** Walk through how to hover over a link to see where it actually leads. Emphasize that display names can lie, but the actual sender domain address doesn't. Show a screenshot of a real-world phishing email with marked callouts.

### Slide 4: Passwords vs. Passphrases
*   **Slide Content:** Visual comparison: `Pa$$w0rd!` vs. `coffee-window-backpack-chair`.
*   **Talking Points:** Explain that computers crack complex but short passwords easily. Introduce the Passphrase method: 4 or more random words. Explain that length is the most important factor in password strength.

### Slide 5: MFA and Password Managers
*   **Slide Content:** What MFA is (Something you know, have, are), benefits of using a password manager.
*   **Talking Points:** MFA is our seatbelt. Even if a hacker gets your password, MFA stops them. A password manager saves you from memorizing passwords and helps prevent phishing because it won't autofill credentials on fake sites.

### Slide 6: Beyond Email: Social Engineering
*   **Slide Content:** Vishing (phone scams), Pretexting, Tailgating (physical security).
*   **Talking Points:** Security isn't just digital. Explain tailgating: someone following you into the building. Explain vishing: attackers calling pretending to be from IT to get MFA codes. Emphasize that saying "no" and asking for verification is the correct security posture.

## 4. Interactive Group Activities

### Activity A: Spot the Phish (10 Minutes)
*   **Objective:** Practice identifying phishing indicators in a group setting.
*   **Instructions:**
    1.  Project two emails on the screen (or print them out for groups of 4-5).
    2.  *Email 1 (Phish):* A spoofed notification from "IT Helpdesk" (`admin@company-security-portal.net`) stating that "Immediate database migration requires password updates. Click here: `http://login.co-secure-migration.com/update`".
    3.  *Email 2 (Legitimate):* A typical newsletter or internal update containing no links requesting logins, sent from the actual corporate domain.
    4.  Ask the audience to call out the red flags they see in Email 1.
*   **Facilitator Cheat Sheet:**
    *   Red Flag 1: The sender address has a domain (`company-security-portal.net`) that doesn't match the company domain.
    *   Red Flag 2: High urgency ("Immediate migration").
    *   Red Flag 3: The link destination domain is unverified and uses `http://` instead of `https://`.
    *   Red Flag 4: Broad generic request for passwords (which IT never does).

### Activity B: The Passphrase Challenge (5 Minutes)
*   **Objective:** Understand how easy it is to generate strong passphrases.
*   **Instructions:**
    1.  Ask the audience to shout out four completely random, unrelated nouns (e.g., "Desk", "Ocean", "Bicycle", "Apple").
    2.  Write them on the whiteboard separated by dashes: `desk-ocean-bicycle-apple`.
    3.  Explain that this simple, memorable sequence contains 24 characters and is virtually uncrackable. Contrast it with a short, complex password that is impossible to memorize.

## 5. FAQ & Employee Objection Bank

### Q1: "Why do I need to use a password manager? Can't I just write them down in a notebook or keep them in an Excel sheet?"
*   **Facilitator Answer:** *"Keeping passwords in an unencrypted Excel sheet or on a physical notebook is risky. If your notebook is lost or a colleague steps near your desk, those credentials are exposed. An approved password manager encrypts your vault, requires MFA to unlock, and automatically fills details for you, which also prevents you from entering passwords on fake phishing sites."*

### Q2: "I am extremely busy. Inspecting every email and hovering over links takes too much time."
*   **Facilitator Answer:** *"It takes only 2 seconds to hover over a link or double-check a sender's address. Contrast that with the days or weeks of downtime and stress that a ransomware attack or security breach causes you and the company. Think of it as a brief health safety check."*

### Q3: "What if I accidentally click a phishing link but close it immediately?"
*   **Facilitator Answer:** *"If you just click and don't enter details or download files, you are usually safe, but it's best to run an IT scan. If you did enter a password or download an attachment, change your password immediately and contact IT Security. We won't punish you for reporting—honesty and speed are what protect us."*

### Q4: "I feel rude refusing to hold the door for someone carrying items into the office."
*   **Facilitator Answer:** *"It feels counter-intuitive to be impolite, but cybersecurity is about protecting everyone in this room. You can be polite and secure: offer to hold the boxes while they tap their badge, or walk them to the front desk reception so the receptionist can sign them in. A legitimate visitor or employee will always understand and respect the policy."*
